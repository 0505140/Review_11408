## 一、全文主旨
### 中文主旨
本文围绕战后日本传统工作道德价值观衰退的核心现象展开，深入分析了该现象出现的三大诱因：战后婴儿潮一代成年与女性进入职场挤压了青少年的发展空间、应试教育体系对创造力与人格培养的长期忽视、城市化带来的生活方式剧变对传统家庭与社群价值观的冲击；同时指出了该现象引发的学生与职场人高不满度、校园暴力频发、离婚率与自杀率大幅攀升等一系列社会问题。
### 英文主旨
This article focuses on the core phenomenon of the decline of traditional work-moral values in post-war Japan, and deeply analyzes three major causes of this phenomenon: the coming of age of the post-war baby boomers and women's entry into the workplace squeezing young people's development space, the long-term neglect of creativity and personality cultivation by the exam-oriented education system, and the impact of dramatic lifestyle changes brought by urbanization on traditional family and community values. It also points out a series of social problems caused by this phenomenon, including high dissatisfaction of students and workers, frequent school violence, and a sharp rise in divorce rate and suicide rate.

## 二、逐段逐句精读解析
### 第一段
#### 逐句翻译、词汇与长难句分析
1. 原文：Aimlessness has hardly been typical of the postwar Japan whose productivity and social harmony are the envy of the United States and Europe.
   - 译文：漫无目标几乎从来都不是战后日本的典型特征，其生产力水平与社会和谐程度令美国和欧洲各国艳羡不已。
   - 常考单词&短语：
     - aimlessness n. 漫无目标，无方向感
     - hardly adv. 几乎不，几乎没有（表否定含义，考研高频考点词）
     - typical adj. 典型的，有代表性的；固定搭配 `be typical of` 是…的典型特征
     - postwar adj. 战后的
     - productivity n. 生产力，生产率
     - social harmony 社会和谐
     - envy n. 羡慕，令人羡慕的人/事物；固定搭配 `the envy of sb` 令某人羡慕的对象
   - 长难句结构分析：
     本句为带限制性定语从句的复合句。主句为 `Aimlessness has hardly been typical of the postwar Japan`；`whose` 引导定语从句，先行词为 `postwar Japan`，`whose` 在从句中作定语修饰 `productivity and social harmony`，从句主干为 `whose productivity and social harmony are the envy of the United States and Europe`。

2. 原文：But increasingly the Japanese are seeing a decline of the traditional work-moral values.
   - 译文：但如今，日本人正愈发见证着传统工作道德价值观的衰退。
   - 常考单词&短语：
     - increasingly adv. 越来越多地，日益
     - see v. 见证，经历；目睹（此处为熟词生义，考研高频考点）
     - decline n. 衰退，下降，衰落
     - traditional adj. 传统的
     - work-moral values 工作道德价值观

3. 原文：Ten years ago young people were hardworking and saw their jobs as their primary reason for being, but now Japan has largely fulfilled its economic needs, and young people don’t know where they should go next.
   - 译文：十年前，年轻人勤奋肯干，将工作视为自己生存的首要意义，但如今，日本的经济需求已基本得到满足，年轻人却不知道自己接下来该何去何从。
   - 常考单词&短语：
     - hardworking adj. 勤奋的，肯干的
     - 固定搭配 `see...as...` 把…视为…
     - primary adj. 首要的，主要的；初级的
     - reason for being 生存的意义，存在的理由
     - largely adv. 很大程度上，基本，主要地
     - fulfill v. 满足，实现；履行
     - economic needs 经济需求
   - 长难句结构分析：
     本句为 `but` 连接的并列复合句。前半分句 `Ten years ago young people were hardworking and saw their jobs as their primary reason for being` 为并列谓语结构，`were hardworking` 与 `saw their jobs as...` 为两个并列的谓语；后半分句由 `and` 连接两个并列句，其中第二个并列句包含 `where` 引导的宾语从句，作 `know` 的宾语。

#### 本段核心概括
本段先通过对比，指出战后日本曾凭借高生产力与社会和谐度被欧美国家艳羡，绝非漫无目标的国家，随后点明全文核心论题——日本传统工作道德价值观正日益衰退，并通过十年前后的今昔对比，说明经济需求基本满足后日本年轻人失去人生方向的现状。

## 三、完整原文
Aimlessness has hardly been typical of the postwar Japan whose productivity and social harmony are the envy of the United States and Europe. But increasingly the Japanese are seeing a decline of the traditional work-moral values. Ten years ago young people were hardworking and saw their jobs as their primary reason for being, but now Japan has largely fulfilled its economic needs, and young people don’t know where they should go next.

The coming of age of the postwar baby boom and an entry of women into the male-dominated job market have limited the opportunities of teen-agers who are already questioning the heavy personal sacrifices involved in climbing Japan’s rigid social ladder to good schools and jobs. In a recent survey, it was found that only 24.5 percent of Japanese students were fully satisfied with school life, compared with 67.2 percent of students in the United States. In addition, far more Japanese workers expressed dissatisfaction with their jobs than did their counterparts in the 10 other countries surveyed.

While often praised by foreigners for its emphasis on the basics, Japanese education tends to stress test taking and mechanical learning over creativity and self-expression. “Those things that do not show up in the test scores — personality, ability, courage or humanity — are completely ignored,” says Toshiki Kaifu, chairman of the ruling Liberal Democratic Party’s education committee. “Frustration against this kind of thing leads kids to drop out and run wild.” Last year Japan experienced 2，125 incidents of school violence, including 929 assaults on teachers. Amid the outcry, many conservative leaders are seeking a return to the prewar emphasis on moral education. Last year Mitsuo Setoyama, who was then education minister, raised eyebrows when he argued that liberal reforms introduced by the American occupation authorities after World War II had weakened the “Japanese morality of respect for parents.”

But that may have more to do with Japanese life-styles. “In Japan,” says educator Yoko Muro, “it’s never a question of whether you enjoy your job and your life, but only how much you can endure.” With economic growth has come centralization; fully 76 percent of Japan’s 119 million citizens live in cities where community and the extended family have been abandoned in favor of isolated, two-generation households. Urban Japanese have long endured lengthy commutes (travels to and from work) and crowded living conditions, but as the old group and family values weaken, the discomfort is beginning to tell. In the past decade, the Japanese divorce rate, while still well below that of the United States, has increased by more than 50 percent, and suicides have increased by nearly one-quarter.
