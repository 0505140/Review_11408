## 完整原文
Text 2

1. Since the dawn of human ingenuity, people have devised ever more cunning tools to cope with work that is dangerous, boring, burdensome, or just plain nasty. That compulsion has resulted in robotics—the science of conferring various human capabilities on machines. And if scientists have yet to create the mechanical version of science fiction, they have begun to come close.

2. As a result, the modern world is increasingly populated by intelligent gizmos whose presence we barely notice but whose universal existence has removed much human labor. Our factories hum to the rhythm of robot assembly arms. Our banking is done at automated teller terminals that thank us with mechanical politeness for the transaction. Our subway trains are controlled by tireless robot-drivers. And thanks to the continual miniaturization of electronics and micro-mechanics, there are already robot systems that can perform some kinds of brain and bone surgery with submillimeter accuracy—far greater precision than highly skilled physicians can achieve with their hands alone.

3. But if robots are to reach the next stage of laborsaving utility, they will have to operate with less human supervision and be able to make at least a few decisions for themselves—goals that pose a real challenge. “While we know how to tell a robot to handle a specific error,” says Dave Lavery, manager of a robotics program at NASA, “we can’t yet give a robot enough ‘common sense’ to reliably interact with a dynamic world.”

4. Indeed the quest for true artificial intelligence has produced very mixed results. Despite a spell of initial optimism in the 1960s and 1970s when it appeared that transistor circuits and microprocessors might be able to copy the action of the human brain by the year 2010, researchers lately have begun to extend that forecast by decades if not centuries.

5. What they found, in attempting to model thought, is that the human brain's roughly one hundred billion nerve cells are much more talented—and human perception far more complicated—than previously imagined. They have built robots that can recognize the error of a machine panel by a fraction of a millimeter in a controlled factory environment. But the human mind can glimpse a rapidly changing scene and immediately disregard the 98 percent that is irrelevant, instantaneously focusing on the monkey at the side of a winding forest road or the single suspicious face in a big crowd. The most advanced computer systems on Earth can't approach that kind of ability, and neuroscientists still don’t know quite how we do it.

## 完整译文
Text 2

1. 自从人类智慧初现以来，人们就设计出越来越精巧的工具，来应对那些危险、枯燥、繁重，或是纯粹令人不快的工作。这种冲动催生了机器人学——一门将人类的各种能力赋予机器的科学。尽管科学家们尚未创造出科幻小说中的机械版智能机器人，但他们已经开始接近这一目标。

2. 因此，现代世界正日益被智能装置所充斥，我们几乎注意不到它们的存在，但它们的普遍存在已经减轻了大量人类劳动。我们的工厂随着机器人装配臂的节奏嗡嗡作响。我们的银行业务在自动柜员终端上完成，这些终端会以机械般的礼貌为交易向我们致谢。我们的地铁列车由不知疲倦的机器人司机操控。而且，得益于电子设备和微机械技术的不断微型化，现在已经出现了能够以亚毫米级精度执行某些脑部和骨科手术的机器人系统——其精度远高于技术娴熟的医生仅凭双手所能达到的水平。

3. 但如果机器人要进入节省劳力的下一个阶段，它们必须能够在更少的人工监督下运行，并且至少能够独立做出一些决定——这些目标构成了真正的挑战。美国国家航空航天局（NASA）机器人项目主管戴夫·拉弗里说：“虽然我们知道如何让机器人处理特定的错误，但我们还无法赋予机器人足够的‘常识’，让它们可靠地与动态变化的世界互动。”

4. 事实上，对真正人工智能的追求产生了喜忧参半的结果。尽管在20世纪60和70年代曾有过一段初期的乐观时期，当时人们认为晶体管电路和微处理器可能在2010年之前就能复制人类大脑的活动，但研究人员最近已开始将这一预测延后了即便不是几个世纪，至少也是几十年。

5. 在尝试模拟人类思维的过程中，他们发现，人类大脑中约一千亿个神经细胞比之前想象的要能干得多，而人类的感知能力也远比想象中复杂得多。他们制造的机器人能够在受控的工厂环境中，识别出机器面板上不足一毫米的误差。但人类的大脑只需扫一眼快速变化的场景，就能立即忽略其中98%无关的信息，瞬间聚焦在蜿蜒林间小路旁的猴子，或是茫茫人海中一张可疑的面孔上。地球上最先进的计算机系统也无法企及这种能力，而神经科学家们至今仍不完全清楚人类是如何做到这一点的。
